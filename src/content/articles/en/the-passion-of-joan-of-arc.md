Dreyer tells the trial and execution of Joan of Arc almost entirely in close-ups. Without
make-up, faces fill the screen until they become moral landscapes: on one side faith, on
the other power.

## Falconetti's face

Renée Jeanne Falconetti's performance is among the most overwhelming in the history of
cinema. Every tear, every tremor of the lip, is filmed with a rawness no word — and the
film is silent — could reach.

> How do you know you are in God's grace? If I am not, may God bring me to it.

## The geometry of power

The judges appear in skewed angles, oppressive framings, spaces that crush. With the
camera, Dreyer builds a tribunal that is, above all, an architecture of humiliation.

## Fire and silence

The final pyre, shot with unbearable realism, seeks not spectacle but communion. *The
Passion of Joan of Arc* proves that cinema, even without sound, could touch the sacred.
