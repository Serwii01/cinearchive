An elderly couple travels to Tokyo to visit their children and discovers there is no
longer any room for them. Ozu tells this minimal story with a precision that turns the
everyday into one of cinema's most moving experiences.

## The low camera

Ozu places the camera at the height of someone seated on the tatami. He doesn't look
down on his characters: he keeps them company. The static frames, the "impossible"
cuts and the empty spaces create a rhythm that breathes like an inhabited house.

> Isn't life disappointing?

## Courtesy as distance

No one shouts. The children are polite, busy, correct. And it is precisely within that
correctness that abandonment lodges itself. Ozu understands that modern indifference
wears no villain's face: it is simply in a hurry.

## What remains

The only one who truly cares for the old couple is the widowed daughter-in-law, bound
by no blood at all. *Tokyo Story* is, at heart, a serene question about what it means
to belong to someone.
