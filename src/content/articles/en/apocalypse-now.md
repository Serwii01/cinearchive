Captain Willard travels up a river to assassinate Colonel Kurtz, an officer who has
become a god in the jungle. Coppola uses that mission as a pretext to film war as what
it is: a bottomless moral hallucination.

## Spectacle and horror

The helicopter assault with Wagner blaring is at once sublime and obscene. Coppola
refuses to separate beauty from terror: he makes us revel, then shames us for having
done so.

> I love the smell of napalm in the morning.

## The river as mind

Each bend of the river draws Willard further from sanity and closer to Kurtz, his
reflection. The film itself — shot amid a legendary chaos — seems to catch the same
fever.

## The horror, the horror

Kurtz is no monster: he is a man who has seen too much and drawn logical conclusions
from an illogical war. His final whisper doesn't close the film: it leaves it open like
a wound.
