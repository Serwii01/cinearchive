A petty thief who thinks he's Humphrey Bogart, an American student selling newspapers on
the Champs-Élysées. Godard takes a film-noir plot and blows it up from within, in the
most insolent film of his generation.

## The jump cut

Godard cuts within a single shot, leaps through time, leaves gaps. What looked like a
beginner's error becomes language: cinema no longer hides its seams, it flaunts them.
Nothing would ever be edited the same way again.

> Informers inform, burglars burgle, but love — love as you please.

## Quotation as breathing

Godard quotes Bogart, Faulkner, painting, himself. Cinema becomes a conversation with all
prior cinema. Culture stops being solemn and turns into play, pose, pulse.

## Betrayal and the street

The ending on a Paris street, with Belmondo falling and Seberg turning to the camera,
seals a truth: the New Wave didn't want to tell stories, it wanted to reinvent how they
are told.
